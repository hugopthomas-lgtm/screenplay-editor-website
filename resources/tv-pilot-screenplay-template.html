TV Pilot Screenplay Template for Google Docs

A free template for hour-long dramas and half-hour comedies

Television pilots follow different rules than feature films. Act breaks, cold opens, teasers, tags—the structure of episodic storytelling requires specific formatting that signals to readers you understand the medium. This template gives you that foundation.

Writing a pilot isn't just about telling a great story in 30 or 60 pages. It's about demonstrating a world that can sustain hundreds of episodes. You need to introduce characters, establish tone, tell a complete story, and plant seeds for seasons to come—all while following television's specific formatting conventions.

Whether you're writing a prestige drama for streaming, a network procedural, or a single-camera comedy, this Google Docs template provides the professional formatting you need. It handles the technical requirements so you can focus on building your series.

TV Pilot-Specific Formatting Tips

Format Your Act Breaks Correctly

Each act break should be centered, in caps: "END OF ACT ONE" followed by a page break. The next page starts with "ACT TWO" centered at the top. This isn't just convention—it tells producers exactly where commercial breaks would fall.

Your Teaser Is Your Pitch

The cold open (or teaser) is the first thing readers see. It should immediately establish tone, hook the audience, and ideally pose a question that carries through the episode. Keep it tight—usually 3-5 pages for drama, 1-3 for comedy.

Introduce Your Ensemble Efficiently

TV pilots need to introduce more characters than features, but you have less time. Use action lines to give quick, memorable character descriptions. Focus on behavior over appearance—what they do tells us who they are.

End on Engines, Not Conclusions

Unlike features, pilots shouldn't wrap everything up. End with engines—ongoing conflicts, relationships in tension, mysteries unsolved. Your pilot needs to prove there's more story to tell. The best pilots make readers immediately want episode two.
