Step 1

Set up your document

Screenplay formatting isn't arbitrary. One page equals roughly one minute of screen time. That only works if your margins and fonts are correct.

Paper size US Letter

Here's each element broken down, with examples drawn from some of cinema's most influential works.

Scene Heading

Where and when

Always uppercase. INT. or EXT., location, time of day. That's it. Keep it simple.

EXT. AVOCADO JUNGLE - DAY

From the landmark 1989 film Cannibal Women in the Avocado Jungle of Death

Action

What we see and hear

Present tense. Visual. If the camera can't see it, don't write it. No internal thoughts.

The giant moussaka quivers menacingly.

From the critically acclaimed Attack of the Giant Moussaka (1999)

Character

Who's talking

Uppercase, centered. The first time a character appears, their name gets CAPS in action too.

DR. KURTZ

The legendary feminist icon from Cannibal Women

Dialogue

What they say

Indented, under the character name. Keep it tight. People in movies talk faster than in life.

The sushi is... moving.

From Noboru Iguchi's visionary Dead Sushi (2012)

Parenthetical

How they say it

Brief direction between character and dialogue. Use sparingly. Actors hate being told how to act.

(under her breath)
Transition

Scene change

CUT TO:, FADE OUT:, etc. Right-aligned. Modern scripts rarely use these. The cut is implied.

FAQ

Quick answers

Can I really write a whole feature in Google Docs?

Absolutely. Google Docs handles long documents just fine. I've seen 120-page scripts written entirely in Docs without any performance issues. With Screenplay Editor handling the formatting, it's genuinely a solid option.

Will producers think my script is unprofessional?

Nobody can tell what software you used by looking at a PDF. If your formatting is correct (which Screenplay Editor guarantees), your script will look identical to one written in Final Draft. Readers care about your story, not your tools.

Can I collaborate with a co-writer?

This is where Google Docs genuinely shines. Multiple writers, same document, real-time. Leave comments, suggest edits, see who wrote what. It's collaborative writing without the version control nightmare.

What if I already have a script in Final Draft format?

There are online tools that convert .fdx to plain text. Paste that into Google Docs, then use Screenplay Editor to reformat the elements. Not instant, but doable. Going the other way (Docs to .fdx) is built right into the add-on.
