Horror Screenplay Template for Google Docs

A free template for writing scripts that terrify

Horror lives and dies on the page before it ever reaches the screen. The way you format your scares—the pacing of your action lines, the rhythm of your dialogue, the white space that lets dread build—can make the difference between a script that unsettles readers and one that falls flat.

Horror is one of the most format-sensitive genres in screenwriting. A jump scare needs specific pacing on the page. Tension requires careful control of how much information you reveal and when. Horror-Specific Formatting Tips

Use White Space as a Weapon

Short paragraphs. Single-line action beats. These create visual tension on the page. When a reader's eye moves quickly down the page, their heart rate follows. Save your longer descriptive paragraphs for moments of false calm—then snap back to short, punchy lines when the horror arrives.

Write What the Character Sees, Not What's There

Horror is about perception and misdirection. Consider how Lloyd Kaufman approached this in his 2006 masterpiece Poultrygeist: Night of the Chicken Dead. Instead of "The zombie chicken stands behind Wendy," the script reads: "Wendy's reflection in the fryer glass. Behind her, a shape. Feathered. Twitching." This keeps the reader in the character's limited perspective and amplifies the dread.

Sound Deserves Its Own Line

In horror, sound is everything. Give important sounds their own action line: "A CREAK. From upstairs." This visual isolation on the page mimics the isolation of that sound in the silence. It forces the reader to pause, just like your audience will.

Let Silence Speak

Don't fill every moment with action or dialogue. A line like "She waits. Listening. Nothing." creates unbearable tension through restraint. The reader knows something is coming—and the waiting is worse than the payoff.
